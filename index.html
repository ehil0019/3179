Left Behind: Health Access and Outcomes in Remote Australia

This visualisation examines how geographic remoteness shapes the availability of health services and the health outcomes of Australians — from who lives where, to where services are located, to the consequences for hospitalisation and mortality.

Section 1 — Context

Who lives where, and what does the population look like?

Australia's population is heavily concentrated along the eastern seaboard and in Perth, with the vast interior home to fewer than 700,000 people across Remote and Very Remote categories combined. The choropleth map shows this distribution by remoteness area.

Remote populations skew younger — but not because they are healthier. Very Remote areas have proportionally more children and working-age adults, and far fewer elderly residents, reflecting shorter life expectancy rather than better health. The age pyramids show this pattern clearly across all five remoteness categories.

Section 3 — Hospitalisation

How does remoteness relate to hospitalisation rates over time?

When primary care is unavailable, manageable conditions escalate until hospital admission becomes unavoidable. The line chart below tracks separation rates per 1,000 people by remoteness area from 2013 to 2024. The gap between Major Cities and Remote areas has persisted — and in some years widened — over the entire period.

Hospital Separations per 1,000 People by Remoteness Area, 2013–2024
AIHW, Australian Hospital Statistics · Hover for values

Remote Australians are hospitalised at nearly twice the rate of city-dwellers, year after year.

Section 4 — Mortality

Which conditions are driving higher death rates in remote areas?

The heatmap shows age-standardised death rates per 100,000 for all conditions appearing in any remoteness area's top 10 causes of death. Grey cells indicate the condition was not a leading cause in that area. Read across each row — for almost every condition, the rate rises with remoteness.

Leading Causes of Death by Remoteness Area — Age-Standardised Rate per 100,000
AIHW, Deaths in Australia, 2025 · Data period: 2021–2023 · Grey = not in top 10 for that area · Hover for values

Dementia declines with remoteness — not because outcomes are better, but because remote populations do not live long enough for it to become a leading cause. Suicide and land transport accidents are absent from Major Cities' top 10, yet prominent in Remote and Very Remote areas.

The slope chart below traces five conditions with the most revealing trajectories. Diabetes is the most striking: an entirely manageable condition whose death rate rises 3.6 times from Major Cities to Very Remote — directly proportional to the absence of primary care.

Death Rate Trajectories Across Remoteness — Selected Conditions
AIHW, Deaths in Australia, 2025 · Age-standardised rate per 100,000 · Dashed lines = conditions not in Major Cities top 10

Diabetes: 15.4 per 100,000 in Major Cities → 55.1 in Very Remote. With access to a GP, it need not be fatal.
