Left Behind: Health Access and Outcomes in Remote Australia

This visualisation examines how geographic remoteness shapes the availability of health services and the health outcomes of Australians — from who lives where, to where services are located, to the consequences for hospitalisation and mortality.

Section 1 — Context

Who lives where, and what does the population look like?

Australia's population is heavily concentrated along the eastern seaboard and in Perth, with the vast interior home to fewer than 700,000 people across Remote and Very Remote categories combined.

Age and Sex Structure by Remoteness Area
ABS, 2021 Census • Percentage of population by age group and sex

Remote populations skew younger, but not because they are healthier. Very Remote areas have proportionally more children and working-age adults, and far fewer elderly residents, reflecting shorter life expectancy. The age pyramids above show this pattern across all five remoteness categories.

The larger 25–34 cohort in Very Remote areas reflects workforce migration and a younger Indigenous population base, not greater wellbeing.

Section 3 — Hospitalisation

How does remoteness relate to hospitalisation rates over time?

When primary care is unavailable, manageable conditions escalate until hospital admission becomes unavoidable. The chart below tracks hospital separation rates per 1,000 people by remoteness area from 2013 to 2024. The gap between Major Cities and Remote areas has persisted, and in some years widened, over the entire period.

Hospital Separations per 1,000 People by Remoteness Area, 2013–2024
AIHW, Australian Hospital Statistics · Hover for values

Very remote Australians are hospitalised at nearly twice the rate of city-dwellers, year after year.

Section 4 — Mortality

Which conditions are driving higher death rates in remote areas?

The heatmap shows age-standardised death rates per 100,000 for all conditions appearing in any remoteness area's top 10 causes of death. Grey cells indicate the condition was not a leading cause in that area. For almost every condition, rates rise with remoteness. The slope chart isolates five conditions with the most revealing trajectories.

Leading Causes of Death by Remoteness Area
AIHW, Deaths in Australia, 2025 • 2021–2023 • Age-standardised rate per 100,000 • Grey = not in top 10

Dementia declines with remoteness as remote populations do not live long enough for it to become a leading cause. Suicide and land transport accidents are absent from Major Cities' top 10, yet prominent in Remote and Very Remote areas.

Death Rate Trajectories — Selected Conditions
AIHW, Deaths in Australia, 2025 • Age-standardised rate per 100,000 • Dashed lines = absent from Major Cities top 10

Diabetes rises from 15.4 per 100,000 in Major Cities to 55.1 in Very Remote. With access to a GP, it does not need to be fatal.
